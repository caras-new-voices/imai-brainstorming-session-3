Meeting context & objectives

The meeting was a cross-functional workshop designed to identify weaknesses, leaks, and opportunities across the company's revenue funnel. The group reviewed multiple stages of the customer journey — and the conversation deliberately connected sales, product, customer success, finance, account management, internal communication, and data infrastructure.

Acquisition Conversion Onboarding Retention Expansion Delight & Advocacy

The workshop set out to determine

  1. Where the company is currently losing revenue.
  2. Which stages of the funnel deserve the most attention.
  3. How those weaknesses should be measured.
  4. What organizational or operational changes may be required.
  5. How enterprise and SMB growth strategies should differ.
"Where does it show in the numbers?"

A recurring principle throughout the session: every perceived problem should eventually be linked to a measurable indicator — converting broad concerns into measurable business problems.

01 Key Discussion Themes

Twelve themes from the room

The discussion clustered around twelve interconnected themes — spanning revenue strategy, product readiness, operations, data, and organizational execution.

1

Protect & improve the existing revenue base

The company's first priority should be improving the area that currently produces the largest amount of revenue.

"This currently is our largest source of revenue… you improve your current source of revenue."

Rather than focusing exclusively on new initiatives, the company should first optimize the revenue stream that is already proven. This could include:

  • Improving conversion within the current customer segment
  • Increasing retention
  • Increasing usage of existing products
  • Expanding customer contracts
  • Reducing service or product friction
  • Identifying the most profitable customer types and use cases
Why it mattersImprovements to a proven revenue source may generate faster and more predictable returns than building an entirely new motion.
2

Enterprise requires both acquisition and conversion

Enterprise growth cannot be reduced to a single funnel stage. The group identified two separate but connected needs: generate more qualified enterprise opportunities, and improve the ability to convert them.

"For enterprise, I think we need acquire and convert."

The problem isn't a lack of market opportunity — the opposite. The enterprise opportunity is substantial but difficult to operationalize.

"There's so much opportunity there… it's just a big old wall that we need to start welcoming people through."

Distinct motions to strategize separately

Stagwell-sourced enterprise Direct enterprise Brand-direct sales Technology-led enterprise Agency relationships Existing-customer expansion

Treating all enterprise leads as a single category may obscure the different requirements of each motion.

3

Expansion should become a core revenue engine

"Ultimately, 80% of revenue is going to come from existing customers. It has to."

The company already has products and capabilities that existing customers are not using.

For SMB customers

  • Limited product growth
  • Limited feature adoption
  • Limited workspace growth
  • Limited structured upselling

For enterprise customers

  • One use case → multiple use cases
  • One workspace → multiple workspaces
  • One department → multiple departments
  • One brand → additional brands
  • One geography → additional markets
Strategic significanceExpansion should not be an opportunistic activity that happens after a successful project — it should be designed into the original customer journey. Sales, onboarding, product configuration, customer success, and account management should all prepare customers for future expansion.
4

Customer delight, advocacy & referrals

The group discussed adding "delight" as a formal stage — the point at which a customer becomes willing to refer, introduce, participate in a case study, allow use of its name or logo, speak publicly, or bring the technology to a future employer.

"They go to another company, they bring their technology with them."

A relationship may be genuinely successful when a customer says yes to a case study, a reference, an introduction, or an expanded relationship.

Important qualificationAdvocacy cannot be created before customer success exists. "We must have acquire and succeed with enterprise clients in order to do the delight part."
5

Rights to use customer names, logos & case studies

The discussion focused on whether InfluencerMarketing.ai can publicly state that it works with P&G and possibly use related brand logos. Potential uses include website logos, sales decks, competitive targeting, enterprise outbound, case studies, social proof, and demand-generation campaigns.

The group correctly identified that contractual permission must be verified first — leading to an explicit follow-up to review the contract and confirm in writing what public claims are permitted (names, logos, individual brands, and which channels).

Parking lotThis was appropriately placed in a "parking lot" rather than allowing the legal-commercial question to derail the broader workshop.
6

Onboarding is a major revenue leak

Ron identified onboarding as his main concern. The customer's first experience has a disproportionate impact on product understanding, confidence, initial adoption, time to value, conversion, retention, and expansion.

Potential onboarding metrics

  • Number of successful onboarding sessions & demos
  • Time from signing to first meaningful product use
  • Time to first completed campaign · time to first insight or result
  • Percentage who complete onboarding · who onboard independently · who require CS intervention
  • Product usage after 20, 30, or 45 days
  • Retention rates segmented by onboarding experience
Key insightOnboarding should not be measured only as an activity completed by the customer-success team. It should be measured by the customer's resulting behavior and progress — and the measurement system must isolate onboarding-related variables rather than relying only on overall retention.
7

Products are launched before they're enterprise-ready

"We launch product very, very quickly, and it's usually 70% of the way there."

Rapid product development is an advantage, but products that perform adequately in early or lower-pressure environments may not withstand enterprise requirements. The resulting pattern:

  1. A product is launched quickly
  2. Enterprise customers expose weaknesses
  3. The product fails to meet expected standards
  4. The company struggles to convert the opportunity
  5. The company struggles to expand the relationship
  6. Revenue opportunities are lost

Where enterprise pressure exposes weakness

ReliabilityData qualityWorkflow maturityUser permissionsReportingIntegrationsScalabilitySecurityImplementation supportCustomer-facing polishRepeatabilityError handlingOperational documentation
Strategic implicationThe company may need different levels of product readiness for different customer segments, and a clear framework for deciding which technologies can be sold to which customer types and at what stage of maturity.
8

Strategic shifts are implemented chaotically

"The process for actualizing strategic shifts [is] chaotic and lacking holistic communication."

A disconnect between leadership decisions and organizational execution — described with the analogy of a cartoon character running off a cliff, with the body falling before the head. Decisions are made centrally, but teams receive different context, act at different speeds, and continue working under old assumptions.

Why it mattersEven good strategy will underperform without a disciplined process for communicating decisions, assigning owners, updating systems, aligning incentives, setting deadlines, measuring adoption, closing old initiatives, and confirming implementation. This is an operating-model problem, not just a communication problem.
9

Account management is not consistently success-based

"Consistent, strategic, success-based account management."

Customer-success and account-management teams lack a sufficiently organized, shared view of customer goals, relationships, activities, usage, results, expansion opportunities, risks, stakeholders, commitments, and account history. Without this, account management becomes reactive rather than strategic.

Success-based account management should include

  • A defined desired outcome for every customer & an agreed success plan
  • Clear milestones · product usage and outcome tracking
  • Periodic business reviews · risk indicators
  • Expansion triggers · referral and advocacy triggers
  • Documented stakeholder relationships · internal ownership

The company may be managing interactions rather than systematically managing customer outcomes.

10

Customer & revenue data is fragmented

"I had to go to at least four different people, and not one of them had access to everything."

A strong signal of fragmented systems, unclear ownership, and limited data accessibility — affecting sales, customer success, account management, finance, leadership, marketing, and product planning. The group discussed Close CRM but emphasized the problem is larger than the software.

"Even if we go to Salesforce, trash in, trash out."

Two connected problems: the current CRM may not support all required workflows, and the company lacks consistent standards for entering, validating, owning, and maintaining data.

Issues mentioned or implied

  • No single source of truth · inconsistent access · incomplete records · incorrect new data
  • Finance does not fully trust CRM figures
  • Sales and customer-success data are not fully aligned
  • Ownership of data quality is unclear · customer activities are not consistently captured
  • Reporting requires manual investigation across multiple people
11

The company lacks a demand-generation model

"We currently don't really have a model for demand generation."

Low-hanging opportunities include thought leadership, webinars, podcasts, value-based and customer-focused content, educational material, and market commentary. But creating content is not enough — content requires an activation and distribution plan.

Required components of a demand-generation model

Defined target audienceClear messagingContent themesChannel strategyDistribution planPaid & organic amplificationExpected reachConversion assumptionsLead captureSales follow-upMeasurement & attributionBudget linked to return
"We put one pound in, we get one pound and one pence back."

The return was illustrative, but the principle holds: marketing investment should be tied to expected measurable outcomes.

12

Enterprise marketing needs clear leadership

"That's why I want a leader for enterprise marketing."

Enterprise demand generation will not emerge consistently without a person responsible for building the strategy, growing the program, aligning content and distribution, defining target audiences, connecting marketing to sales, measuring results, managing budget, and maintaining momentum.

Resource + accountabilityWithout clear leadership, enterprise marketing may remain a collection of disconnected initiatives rather than a repeatable growth engine.
02 Strategic Insights

Reading between the themes

Seven cross-cutting insights that reframe how the individual themes connect — and where the leverage really sits.

1 · The funnel is not a sequence of independent stages

Acquisition, conversion, onboarding, retention, expansion, and advocacy are deeply interconnected: poor product readiness reduces conversion; poor onboarding reduces retention; poor retention eliminates expansion; weak success prevents case studies; a lack of case studies weakens acquisition. A cross-functional revenue operating model is needed.

2 · Expansion should begin during the initial sale

The company thinks about expansion mainly after adoption. A stronger model defines the expansion path during the original sales process — initial use case, adjacent use cases, additional departments, brands, workspaces, geographies, and the stakeholders and conditions required.

3 · Enterprise readiness is a commercial capability

"70%-complete" is about more than technical functionality. Enterprise readiness also includes sales positioning, customer expectations, implementation, support, security, contracts, reporting, account management, escalation, and success measurement. Establish an enterprise-readiness gate before broad enterprise sale.

4 · Advocacy can compound into acquisition

Delighted enterprise customers provide referrals, introductions, case studies, brand credibility, website proof, conference appearances, competitive differentiation, and future relationships when executives change companies. Advocacy should have measurable goals, ownership, and process.

5 · Data fragmentation blocks strategy and execution

Needing multiple people to assemble basic information suggests leadership lacks a reliable operating view — affecting forecasting, prioritization, expansion, risk management, segmentation, product planning, and financial reconciliation. Governance must precede any new system.

6 · Content creation is being confused with demand generation

A podcast, webinar, or article is not demand generation by itself. It requires targeting, distribution, conversion, follow-up, attribution, and optimization. Invest heavily in formats only once you know how they'll reach and influence the right audience.

7

Strategic volatility may be producing hidden costs

Frequent or poorly communicated strategic shifts can create duplicated work, conflicting priorities, lost employee time, inconsistent customer messaging, broken reporting, delayed execution, reduced accountability, and difficulty measuring performance.

The "highs and lows" in company metrics may not result only from market conditions — they may partially reflect internal execution instability.

This is an inferred insight based on the discussion.

06 Customer, Market, Product & Positioning

Reading the market — and the product

Insights about enterprise buyers, the expansion opportunity inside existing accounts, product-operating principles, and the messaging that could carry the enterprise narrative.

Customer & market insights

Enterprise expectations

Reliable technology, clear onboarding, repeatable implementation, strong reporting, professional support, security & governance, business outcomes, multi-team scalability, clear ownership, and long-term partnership.

Buying dynamics

Enterprise sales are relationship-driven and reputation-sensitive. Customer proof significantly affects conversion — buyers want evidence that similar large organizations trust the company.

Advocacy dynamics

Senior marketers change employers often, and a strong relationship travels with the individual. The individual champion is almost as strategically important as the current corporate account.

Existing-customer opportunityCustomers may be unaware of additional products, use cases, new features, multi-workspace capabilities, and enterprise-wide applications. Part of the expansion problem may be a product-education and account-planning problem rather than a lack of demand.

  • Usage after onboarding · activation milestones

WorkspacesTeamsBrandsMarketsCampaignsBusiness units

Customer-success visibility

Messaging & positioning

Around expansion
